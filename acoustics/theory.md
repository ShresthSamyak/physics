# 1. Basics of Acoustics and Sound Waves

## Definition

Acoustics comes from the Greek word **“akoustikos”** meaning *for hearing*.

Acoustics deals with:

- production of sound
- propagation of sound
- transmission of sound
- detection of sound waves

---

## Classification of Sound

Sound waves are classified based on **frequency**.

| Type | Frequency Range | Example |
|-----|-----|-----|
| Infrasonic | Below 20 Hz | Earthquakes, elephants |
| Audible | 20 Hz – 20,000 Hz | Music, speech |
| Ultrasonic | Above 20,000 Hz | Medical imaging, sonar |

---

## Nature of Sound

Sound is a **mechanical wave**.

This means:

- it **requires a material medium**
- it **cannot travel in vacuum**

Examples of media:

- air
- water
- solids

---

## Interaction of Sound with Materials

When sound hits a material surface, three things may happen.

### Reflection
Sound bounces back.

Examples:
- ceramic tiles
- wood
- metal

---

### Absorption
Sound energy converts into **heat**.

Examples:
- textiles
- curtains
- fibre materials

---

### Transmission
Sound passes through the material.

Examples:
- brick walls
- concrete

---

# 2. Decibel Levels (dB)

Sound intensity is measured using the **decibel (dB)** scale.

---

## Key Properties

- 0 dB represents the **threshold of hearing**
- the scale is **logarithmic**

If a sound is:


10^p times stronger → it is represented as 10p dB


---

## Examples of Sound Levels

| Sound | Approx dB |
|------|------|
| Breathing | 10 dB |
| Normal conversation | 60 dB |
| Busy traffic | 80 dB |
| Shouting in ear | 110 dB |
| Thunder | 120 dB |

---

## Health Hazards

| Sound Level | Effect |
|------|------|
| 85 dB | Must raise voice to talk |
| 90 dB | Ear damage after long exposure |
| 140 dB | Immediate ear damage |

---

# 3. Architectural Acoustics

Architectural acoustics deals with **designing halls and rooms** so that sound quality is optimal.

Examples:

- theatres
- lecture halls
- cinemas
- auditoriums
- concert halls

---

## Causes of Poor Acoustics

A room has poor acoustics if:

- sound intensity is **not uniform**
- frequencies **interfere**
- echoes occur
- reverberation is excessive

---

## Features of a Good Hall

A well-designed hall must satisfy:

- uniform sound distribution
- sufficient loudness
- no echo
- optimum reverberation
- no sound focusing
- protection from external noise

---

# 4. Echo vs Reverberation

## Echo

The human ear retains sound for approximately:


0.1 seconds


If reflected sound arrives **after 0.1 seconds**, we hear it separately.

This is called **echo**.

---

## Distance Condition for Echo

If sound speed ≈ 340 m/s

Minimum distance:


≈ 17 meters


So echoes occur when reflecting surfaces are **more than 17 m away**.

---

## Reverberation

Reverberation occurs when sound reflects repeatedly from walls.

The listener hears:

1. direct sound
2. multiple reflected sounds

This causes **prolongation of sound** even after the source stops.

---

## Reverberation Time

Reverberation time is defined as:

> Time required for sound intensity to reduce to **one millionth** of its original value.

Mathematically:


I = I₀ / 10⁶


---

## Effects of Reverberation

### Too Low

- sound fades quickly
- speech becomes weak

### Too High

- overlapping sounds
- speech becomes unclear

---

## Ideal Reverberation Time

| Hall Type | Reverberation Time |
|------|------|
| Cinema | 1.3 s |
| Lecture hall | ~1 s |
| Church | 1.8 – 3 s |

---

# 5. Anechoic Chambers

An **anechoic chamber** is a room where:

- there are **no echoes**
- there is **no reverberation**

All sound is absorbed.

---

## Characteristics

- walls lined with absorbent wedges
- extremely quiet environment

---

## Example

The anechoic chamber at **Microsoft headquarters** holds the record for the **quietest room on Earth**:


-20.6 dB

# 6. Sabine's and Eyring's Theories

These theories relate to **reverberation time** in halls.

---

## Sabine's Conclusions

Reverberation time depends on:

- volume of hall
- absorbing surfaces
- reflecting properties
- sound frequency

Reverberation time **does NOT depend on**:

- shape of room
- position of source
- position of listener

---

## Limitation of Sabine Formula

Sabine formula is valid only when:


α < 0.2


If a room is perfectly absorbing:


α = 1


Then reverberation time should be:


T = 0


But Sabine formula incorrectly predicts a non-zero value.

---

## Eyring Formula

Eyring formula corrects Sabine's limitation.

For highly absorbent rooms:


α > 0.2


Eyring correctly predicts:


T = 0 when α = 1


---

# 7. Absorption of Sound

Sound absorption occurs when sound energy converts to:

- heat
- vibration energy

---

## Causes of Absorption

### 1. Porosity

Fibrous materials contain pores.

Sound causes air friction inside pores → heat.

Examples:

- wool
- foam
- fibre boards

---

### 2. Flexural Vibration

Flexible surfaces vibrate when struck by sound.

Energy is lost due to damping.

Examples:

- plywood
- thin panels

---

## Absorption Coefficient

Absorption coefficient:


α = absorbed energy / incident energy


Range:


0 ≤ α ≤ 1


---

## Unit of Absorption

Standard unit:


Open Window Unit (OWU)


or


Sabin


Definition:

> One sabin is the sound absorbed by **1 square foot of a fully open window**.

---

# 8. Hall Design and Structure

Proper design improves sound distribution.

---

## Shape of Hall

Best design:


Rectangular "shoebox" shape


Stage located at one narrow end.

---

## Side Wall Design

Side walls should be **angled outward**.

Angle range:


30° – 60°


This improves sound dispersion.

---

## Surfaces and Walls

### Avoid round or elliptical walls

They focus sound like mirrors.

Result:

- uneven sound intensity

---

### Avoid perfectly parallel walls

They create:


standing waves


This causes acoustic resonance.

---

### Preferred Design

Use **angled or irregular walls**.

This spreads sound evenly.

---

# 9. Ceilings and Floors

## Ceiling Design

Use **ceiling reflectors ("clouds")**.

Purpose:

- direct sound toward audience

Sometimes ceilings use **absorbent materials** to prevent late echoes.

---

## Floor Design

Floors should:

- slope toward the stage
- angle ≥ **8°**

Carpet flooring is recommended.

Purpose:

- reduce sound reflection
- prevent echelon effect

---

# 10. Controlling Reverberation and Noise

## Methods to Adjust Reverberation

- openable windows
- heavy curtains
- absorbent wall panels
- carpets
- full audience (people absorb sound)

---

## Methods to Maintain Loudness

- sounding boards behind speaker
- wooden reflecting surfaces above speaker
- relatively low ceilings

---

## Noise Insulation Methods

External noise can be reduced using:

- double-wall construction
- air gaps in walls
- double glass windows
- acoustic insulation materials
- rubber or carpet flooring

---

## Noise Control for Machines

Machines like:

- lifts
- generators
- typewriters

should be placed inside **soundproof cabins**.
