# Oscillations & Waves — Core Concepts

Concept summary for **UPH013 Physics: Oscillations and Waves**

---

# 1. Fundamentals of Periodic Motion and Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

## Periodic Motion
Periodic motion is a repeating motion where an object returns to a given position after a fixed time interval.

**Examples**
- Pendulum motion
- Beach ball floating on water waves

---

## Oscillations
Oscillations refer specifically to **back-and-forth motion about an equilibrium position**.

---

## Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

Simple Harmonic Motion is a **special type of periodic motion** and acts as the **basic building block for complex periodic motions**.

SHM helps explain:

- Mechanical waves
- Sound waves (air particles oscillate)
- Water waves (water particles oscillate)

---

## Condition for SHM

A system performs SHM when:

\[
\text{Acceleration} \propto -(\text{displacement})
\]

or

\[
a \propto -x
\]

This means acceleration is **proportional to displacement and directed toward equilibrium**.

---

## Simple Harmonic Oscillator

A **simple harmonic oscillator** is a system where:

\[
F = -kx
\]

The restoring force is **proportional to displacement and acts toward equilibrium**.

---

## Important Terms

### Equilibrium Position
The point where the system is **neither stretched nor compressed**.

### Displacement
Distance of the object **from equilibrium**.

### Amplitude
Maximum displacement from equilibrium.

### Cycle
One complete **to-and-fro motion**.

### Period (T)
Time required to complete **one full oscillation**.

### Frequency (f)
Number of oscillations per second.

\[
f = \frac{1}{T}
\]

---

## Simple Pendulum

A **simple pendulum** consists of:
- a mass
- attached to a light string

Assumptions:
- string is massless
- string does not stretch

A pendulum exhibits SHM **only for small angular displacements**.

---

# 2. Energy Conservation in Oscillations

In an **ideal frictionless system**, total mechanical energy remains constant.

\[
E = KE + PE
\]

Energy continuously transforms between:

- **Potential Energy**
- **Kinetic Energy**

---

## At Maximum Amplitude

- Velocity = 0
- Kinetic Energy = 0
- All energy is **Potential Energy**

---

## At Equilibrium

- Velocity = maximum
- Potential Energy = minimum
- All energy is **Kinetic Energy**

---

# 3. Electrical Oscillators

An **LC circuit** (Inductor + Capacitor) behaves as an electrical oscillator.

The system allows **charge and current to oscillate**.

---

## Energy Storage in LC Circuit

### Capacitor (Electric Field)

Energy stored:

\[
U_C = \frac{Q^2}{2C}
\]

Analogous to **spring potential energy**.

---

### Inductor (Magnetic Field)

Energy stored:

\[
U_L = \frac{1}{2}LI^2
\]

Analogous to **kinetic energy of a moving mass**.

---

## Ideal LC Circuit

If resistance = 0

- No energy loss
- Oscillations continue forever
- Total energy remains constant

---

# 4. Damped Harmonic Motion

Damped motion occurs when **friction or drag forces act on the oscillator**.

Examples:
- Air resistance
- Fluid resistance
- Electrical resistance

Because of damping:

- Energy decreases with time
- Amplitude gradually reduces
- Oscillation eventually stops

---

## Natural Frequency

The frequency when **no damping force is present**.

---

## Damped Electrical Oscillator (LCR Circuit)

Adding resistance \(R\) introduces damping.

There exists a **critical resistance**:

- Above this value → oscillations stop.

---

# 5. Types of Damping

## Underdamping
- Oscillations occur
- Amplitude gradually decreases

## Critical Damping
- System returns to equilibrium **in the shortest time**
- No oscillation

## Overdamping
- System returns slowly
- No oscillation

---

## Applications of Damping

### Desired Applications

- Automobile shock absorbers
- Mechanical stabilizers

### Undesired Applications

- Clocks
- Watches
- Precision oscillators

---

# 6. Measurements of Damping

## Logarithmic Decrement

Measures **rate of amplitude decay**.

\[
\delta = \ln\left(\frac{x_n}{x_{n+1}}\right)
\]

---

## Relaxation Time

Time required for amplitude to reduce to:

\[
\frac{1}{e}
\]

of the initial value.

---

## Quality Factor (Q)

Quality factor measures **how lightly damped an oscillator is**.

\[
Q =
2\pi
\frac{\text{Energy Stored}}
{\text{Energy Lost per Cycle}}
\]

Higher \(Q\):

- less damping
- longer oscillations

---

# 7. Practical Application — Eddy Currents

## Eddy Currents

Eddy currents are **circulating currents induced in a conductor when exposed to changing magnetic flux**.

These currents produce **magnetic braking forces**.

---

## Effects

- Generate drag force
- Convert kinetic energy to heat

---

## Applications

### Magnetic Braking Systems
Used in:

- subway trains
- rapid transit systems

### Safety Mechanisms

Used in:

- power tools
- rotating machinery

to **stop blades quickly when turned off**.
