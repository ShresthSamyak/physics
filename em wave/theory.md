# 1. Fundamentals & Maxwell's Equations

## Helmholtz Theorem

Helmholtz theorem states:

> If a field vanishes far from its source, knowing its **divergence** and **curl** is enough to completely determine that field everywhere.

This idea forms the mathematical foundation for electromagnetic field theory.

---

## Maxwell's Equations

Electromagnetic waves arise from four fundamental laws:

### 1. Gauss's Law (Electric)

Relates electric flux to enclosed charge.

Key idea:

- Electric charges produce electric fields.

---

### 2. Gauss's Law (Magnetic)

States that:

- **Magnetic monopoles do not exist.**
- Magnetic field lines always form **closed loops**.

---

### 3. Ampère's Law

Relates magnetic fields to:

- electric current
- changing electric fields

This equation explains how **moving charges generate magnetic fields**.

---

### 4. Faraday's Law

States that:

> A **changing magnetic field induces an electric field.**

This is the basic principle behind:

- electric generators
- transformers
- induction devices

---

# 2. Electromagnetic Waves in Vacuum

## Vacuum Conditions

In a perfect vacuum:

- Charge density


ρ = 0


- Current density


J = 0


There are **no free charges and no currents**.

---

## Propagation of EM Waves

Solutions of Maxwell's equations show that:

- Electric field **E**
- Magnetic field **B**

vary in space and time and propagate together as a wave.

---

## Speed of Electromagnetic Waves

The speed of EM waves is


v = 1 / √(μ₀ ε₀)


Substituting constants:


v = 3 × 10⁸ m/s


This is exactly the **speed of light**.

Therefore:

> **Light is an electromagnetic wave.**

---

# 3. Transverse Nature of EM Waves

## Orthogonality

From Maxwell equations:


∇ · E = 0

∇ · B = 0


These relations show that:

- Electric field **E**
- Magnetic field **B**

are both perpendicular to the direction of propagation **k**.

---

## Mutual Perpendicularity

Using curl equations:

- **E is perpendicular to B**
- **B is perpendicular to k**

Thus:


E ⟂ B ⟂ k


---

## Conclusion

Since the fields oscillate **perpendicular to the direction of wave travel**, EM waves are:

> **Transverse waves**

---

## Intrinsic Impedance of Free Space

The ratio of electric field to magnetic field in vacuum is constant.


Z = E / H


This constant is called **intrinsic impedance**.

For vacuum:


Z = 377 Ω


---

# 4. EM Waves in a Conducting Medium

## Conducting Conditions

In conducting materials:


ρ = 0


but


J ≠ 0


because current exists.

Current density relation:


J = σE


Where:

- σ = electrical conductivity

---

## Modified Wave Equation

Because of conductivity, Maxwell's equations gain an **extra term**.

This term accounts for **energy loss due to electrical resistance**.

---

## Complex Wave Vector

The wave vector becomes complex:


k = a + ib


Where:

- **a** = phase constant
- **b** = attenuation constant

---

# 5. Phase Lag and Attenuation

## Phase Shift

In vacuum:

- Electric and magnetic fields are **in phase**.

In conductors:

- They are **not perfectly synchronized**.

There is a phase difference between them.

For **good conductors**:


phase shift = π / 4


or


45°


---

## Attenuation (Exponential Decay)

Inside conductors, wave amplitude decreases exponentially.

This means:

- the deeper the wave travels
- the weaker it becomes

This process is called **attenuation**.

---

# 6. Skin Depth (Penetration Depth)

## Definition

Skin depth (δ) is the distance inside a conductor where the amplitude becomes:


1/e


of its original value.

This is roughly **one-third of the initial amplitude**.

---

## Skin Depth Formula


δ = 1 / b


Where **b** is the attenuation constant.

---

## Frequency Dependence

For good conductors:


δ ∝ 1 / √f


This means:

- Higher frequency → smaller penetration depth
- Lower frequency → deeper penetration

---

# 7. Skin Effect (AC vs DC)

### Direct Current (DC)

Frequency:


f = 0


DC current flows through the **entire cross-section** of the conductor.

---

### Alternating Current (AC)

High frequency AC cannot penetrate deeply.

Most current flows only on the **surface of the conductor**.

This phenomenon is called:

> **Skin Effect**
